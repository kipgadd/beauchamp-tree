The Beauchamp family Line

A summary of thirty-one generations
from a Domesday baron to a present-day household

After Lorraine Shoemaker · expanded with primary-source research
I · NORMAN ORIGINS

The first generation, c. 1066

The line begins with Hugh de Beauchamp, a Norman tenant-in-chief who came to England in the wake of the Conquest. By the time of the Domesday survey of 1086 he held forty-three manors in Bedfordshire alone, with additional estates in Buckinghamshire and Hertfordshire — most taken from the Anglo-Saxon Eskil of Ware. He held Bedford Castle. His name appears on a charter of William the Conqueror as "sheriff." His wife Adeliza Taillebois was the daughter of a Hastings veteran and, through her mother Lucy of Bolingbroke, descended from the Mercian house of Earl Leofric and Lady Godiva.

By the second generation the family had moved west into Worcestershire. Walter de Beauchamp of Elmley — one of King Henry I's "new men" — married Emmeline d'Abetot, daughter of the notoriously harsh Sheriff Urse d'Abetot, and through her acquired both the d'Abetot lands and the hereditary Sheriffdom of Worcestershire. Elmley Castle would remain the family's principal seat for the next 158 years.

II · THE EARLDOM OF WARWICK

From regional barons to the high nobility of England

Six Worcestershire generations later, in 1268, the Beauchamps inherited one of the half-dozen great earldoms of medieval England. William III de Beauchamp had married Isabel Mauduit, sister of the childless 8th Earl of Warwick; on the earl's death the title passed through her to her son. The Beauchamp Earls of Warwick would hold it for the next 178 years.

Two figures from this stretch reach into the broadest English history. Guy de Beauchamp, 10th Earl (1272–1315) — known to Edward II as "the Black Dog of Arden" — personally captured Piers Gaveston in 1312 and ordered his beheading at Blacklow Hill. From Guy's eldest son Thomas Beauchamp, 11th Earl, came one of the original Knights of the Garter, a commander at Crécy and Poitiers, and the rebuilder of Caesar's and Guy's Towers at Warwick Castle. Two generations later, Richard Beauchamp, 13th Earl served as Henry V's commander at Agincourt and as tutor to the infant Henry VI; he is the figure depicted in the magnificent Beauchamp Pageant in the British Library, and his gilt-bronze tomb at Warwick is one of the masterpieces of English funerary art.

The senior earldom ran on for one more generation before the Wars of the Roses caught it. Anne Beauchamp, daughter of Richard the 13th Earl, married Richard Neville — "Warwick the Kingmaker" — and saw both her husband and her son-in-law die at Barnet and Tewkesbury in 1471. Her own daughter Anne Neville became Queen of England as the wife of Richard III; their son Edward of Middleham, the only person of Beauchamp blood ever to be heir apparent to the English crown, died at Middleham aged about ten in 1484. With Bosworth a year later the Yorkist dynasty ended, and Beauchamp blood went no further on the throne — through that line.

III · THE TUDOR LINK

How a Beauchamp granddaughter became ancestress of every monarch since Henry VIII

What the senior line lost at Bosworth, a cadet line had already won by another route. The Beauchamps of Bletso — a Bedfordshire branch — married into the legitimised line of John of Gaunt: Margaret Beauchamp of Bletso (c. 1410–1482) had a daughter, Lady Margaret Beaufort, who in turn bore Henry VII, the founder of the Tudor dynasty.

Through that single Beauchamp grandmother, every English (and later British) monarch from Henry VIII forward — Edward VI, Mary I, Elizabeth I, James I, Charles I and II, William III and Anne, the Hanoverians, Victoria, the Windsors, Elizabeth II, and Charles III — descends. The Beauchamp line that did not survive on the throne in 1485 took a back-door route through the Tudors and never left.

IV · POWICK AND BRIDESHEAD

The cadet line that became a novel

This family's direct descent does not run through the senior Earls of Warwick or through the Bletso line. It runs through a third branch, the Beauchamps of Powick, founded by a younger son of the senior Worcestershire stock and producing — across the 14th and 15th centuries — a Speaker of the House of Commons, a Treasurer of England under Henry VI, and finally an heiress: Anne Beauchamp, who in the late fifteenth century married Sir Thomas Lygon of Madresfield Court.

Three centuries later, in 1815, William Lygon was created Earl Beauchamp in explicit recognition of that descent — formally reviving the Beauchamp surname through Anne. The 7th Earl Beauchamp (1872–1938) — Knight of the Garter, Governor of New South Wales, Liberal Leader of the House of Lords — was driven into European exile in 1931 when his brother-in-law the Duke of Westminster exposed his homosexuality to King George V. The 7th Earl's second son, Hugh Patrick Lygon, was at Oxford with Evelyn Waugh and is the principal real-life model for Lord Sebastian Flyte in Brideshead Revisited. Madresfield itself supplied many features of the fictional Brideshead Castle. The Marchmains and Flytes of twentieth-century English literature are this family's distant cousins.

V · COSGROVE AND THE MAYFLOWER

From Northamptonshire yeomanry to the Plymouth Colony

The line surfaces from medieval obscurity in 1550 with Thomas Beauchamp of Cosgrove, Northamptonshire — a yeoman recorded in the Heralds' Visitation of London in 1633. His youngest son John Beauchamp (c. 1592–1655) left for the City of London, was apprenticed as a salter, and became one of the Merchant Adventurers who financed the Mayflower in 1620. When the Plymouth Colony's debts came due, John was one of five English "Undertakers" who took on the colony's £1,800 obligation in 1626 in exchange for a six-year monopoly on the beaver-fur trade. The arrangement deteriorated; John eventually sued William Bradford in 1641. He never crossed the Atlantic himself. His brother-in-law Edmund Freeman did — sailing on the Abigail in 1635, founding Sandwich on Cape Cod two years later, and lying buried still in East Sandwich beneath two fieldstones called the Saddle and Pillion.

VI · THE EASTERN SHORE

The first Clerk of Somerset County, Maryland

John Beauchamp's son Edmund Beauchamp (1625–1691) crossed in his thirties. On 22 August 1666, by proclamation of Lord Baltimore, Somerset County, Maryland was established. Edmund became its first Clerk and Keeper of Records, a post he held for twenty-five years; the folklorist Stith Thompson, his descendant, called his court books "a more splendidly kept set of records of court proceedings and deeds cannot, we believe, be found elsewhere during the early colonial period."

Eleven days after his marriage to Sarah Dixon, daughter of an early Quaker settler, Edmund purchased 300 acres at the head of the Great Annemessex River — a tract called Contention. He conveyed it to Sarah as her jointure that October. Contention would remain Beauchamp land for more than a century, the family seat through three generations. His will was misplaced in the courthouse for centuries and only rediscovered in an alcove at Princess Anne in 1932.

The next six generations stayed close to the Eastern Shore. Edward (c. 1683–1750) married into the Huguenot Fountain family. His son Fountain Beauchamp served the Continental Army cause as a recognised D.A.R. patriot. Thomas (1716–1759) married into the prominent Handy planter family. Levin Henry Beauchamp (1812–1887) enlisted in the 1st Maryland Cavalry on the Union side in September 1864 and was appointed Postmaster of Westover in 1874. His grandson Francis Tubman Beauchamp and his wife May Davis both died young, in 1896 and 1899 respectively — leaving two small children, Francis Elmo and Margaret, to be raised by Hayman cousins.

VII · THE RAILROAD AND PENNSYLVANIA

An orphan, an 8:57 train, a Vermont town

The orphan boy, Francis Elmo Beauchamp (1893–1973), grew up in the Hayman household on the Eastern Shore and went to work for the Pennsylvania Railroad — Middle Division — as a fireman. On 15 September 1913, at half past six in the morning, he married Regina Catherine Calhoun at St. Thomas's Catholic Church in Bedford, Pennsylvania. The newlyweds caught the 8:57 train for a honeymoon trip to Philadelphia, New York, and Baltimore, then settled their family in Altoona, the great Pennsylvania Railroad shop town in the central highlands.

Their daughter Frances Mae Beauchamp (1916–1975) married William E. Ansman, an Altoona bookkeeper of Pennsylvania-German stock; they later moved west across the state to Butler, north of Pittsburgh. Their daughter Carole Ansman married Geoffrey "Geoff" B. Gaddis — son of Harry Lloyd Gaddis Jr. and Thelma Grace Hicks of Butler — and in the early 1980s they left Pennsylvania for Putney, Vermont, where they spent the rest of their lives in deep civic engagement, supporting Vermont Humanities and the Yellow Barn international chamber music center.

VIII · THE MATERNAL SIDE

St. Joseph and Benton Harbor, Michigan

The maternal line that joins this story comes from southwest Michigan, on the eastern shore of Lake Michigan opposite Chicago. Patricia Fehlberg Carlson raised ten children in a single Patricia-centered household — one (Nancy Bowes) by Verrill Norwood and the other nine, including Beth Carlson-Gaddis, by Gordon Carlson Jr. Beth's mother Patricia descended on one side from Fred Fehlberg and Blossom Pschigoda Fehlberg, whose own family came from the Pschigoda sibling group of William Pschigoda and Emma "Betty" Schultz — Polish-German immigrants to the Berrien County industrial belt. Blossom is honoured today by the Blossom Fehlberg Metal Studio at Water Street Glassworks in Benton Harbor, opened in 2009 by Nancy Bowes in her grandmother's memory.

IX · THE PRESENT GENERATION

Vermont and Rochester, twenty-first century

Carole and Geoff's son Jason Gaddis took an M.Arch. from Yale in 1999 and runs J Gaddis Design, a practice serving the Upper Valley of Vermont and New Hampshire with a focus on historic preservation and universal design. He married Beth Carlson, the affineur — master cheesemaker — at Vermont Shepherd in Putney, whose work helped elevate the Vermont artisan-cheese renaissance.

Their elder son Kip Gaddis is studying mechanical engineering at the Rochester Institute of Technology, with graduation expected in 2027. He is an undergraduate researcher at the RIT Nano Power Research Laboratories, working on a NASA-funded effort to build radioisotope thermoradiative cells for deep-space power generation in collaboration with NASA Glenn. Earlier in 2025 he was a process-engineering intern at the RIT Semiconductor & Nanofabrication Laboratory. He has been part of RIT Formula SAE Racing for three years — first as Aerodynamics Design Engineer, more recently as Electronics Design & Test Engineer — and is a UPWARDS for the Future Fellow under RIT's partnership with Tokyo Electron. Brother: Asa Gaddis.
