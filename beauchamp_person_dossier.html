Bernard "the Dane"

Norman co-regent · semi-legendary
c. 880 – c. 945/955
Direct linespeculativeRelation: speculative 33rd-great-grandfather (traditional only; rejected by modern scholarship)
Connections
  • Spouse: Wife of Bernard (unknown)
  • Outgoing links: Torfin "Karl" d'Harcourt (possible parent)
Notes
The **upper limit of the Harcourt traditional pedigree**. Bernard appears as a major figure in **Dudo of Saint-Quentin's c. 1015 history of the early Norman dukes**, where he is described as a Danish-origin warrior of Rouen, councillor to William Longsword, and **co-regent of Normandy during Richard I's minority** after William's assassination in 942. The 17th-century French genealogist **Gilles-André de La Roque** (in his 1662 _Histoire généalogique de la maison de Harcourt_) made Bernard the patriarch of the d'Harcourt family — and from him every printed Harcourt and Beauchamp pedigree since has traced its origin. **The modern scholarly view, however, is skeptical:** Leah Shopkow (_History and Community: Norman Historical Writing_, 1997) argues Bernard is likely a Dudo literary invention — a stock "wise Danish counsellor"; the contemporary chronicler **Flodoard of Reims**, writing in detail through the same period, never once mentions him; **Charles Cawley's FMG Medieval Lands** explicitly rejects the claim that Bernard was father of Torf le Riche (Torfin Karl), calling La Roque's "Preuves" undated and unverifiable. The honest position: _Bernard the Dane may have been real but his connection to the Harcourt and Beauchamp lines is a 17th-century reconstruction, not history._ **This is where the chain genuinely ends.** Going beyond Bernard — to Hastein, Rollo, Rognvald Eysteinsson, or further Viking ancestors — is not supported by any Harcourt or Beauchamp tradition; those names appear in unrelated saga material or pure 18th-19th c. genealogical fabrication. The 33-generation gap from Kip to Bernard (if any of this holds) represents _about 1,150 years of unbroken family memory_, half of it now provably reconstructed rather than recorded.
Sources
Dudo of Saint-Quentin, _De moribus et actis primorum Normanniae ducum_ (c. 1015); Gilles-André de La Roque, _Histoire généalogique de la maison de Harcourt_ (1662). Scholarly skepticism: Leah Shopkow, _History and Community_ (1997); Charles Cawley, _Medieval Lands_ (FMG); Pierre Bauduin, _La première Normandie_ (2004).

Notes
Identified in the Geneanet tree as Hugh's father. Geneanet places his birthplace at _Beauchamp, Calvados_ — but the regional Norman authority **Wikimanche** and 19th-century herald J.R. Planché both place the family seat at **Beauchamps in La Manche** (canton La Haye-Pesnel, in the Avranchin, between Avranches and Granville), _not_ in the Bessin near Bayeux. The Manche commune is described by Wikimanche as _le berceau anglo-normand des Beauchamp devenus comtes de Warwick et de Worcester_ — the Anglo-Norman cradle of the Beauchamps who became Earls of Warwick and Worcester; 1066.co.nz adds the conjecture that the family was a cadet branch of the **Barons of Saint-Denis-le-Gast**. This Robert is therefore _speculative family tradition rather than primary-source history_. Under the competing **Wikimanche identification** (which conflates Hugues I de Beauchamps with our Hugh of Bedford as one person), no separate Robert generation is needed — Hugh himself is the Hastings combatant. The Robert generation is shown here only because the Geneanet family tradition records it; the regional Norman tradition does not.

Hugh / Hugues I de Beauchamp

Seigneur de Beauchamps · 1st Feudal Baron of Bedford
c. 1040 – after 1100
Direct linetraditionalRelation: 28th-great-grandfather
Connections
  • Spouse: Adeliza Taillebois
  • Incoming links: Robert de Beauchamp (possible parent)
  • Outgoing links: Walter de Beauchamp (parent/child)
Notes
Norman tenant-in-chief; held **43 manors in Bedfordshire** at the Domesday survey of 1086 plus holdings in Buckinghamshire and Hertfordshire. Held Bedford Castle. Witnessed a charter of William the Conqueror as "sheriff" between 1070 and 1089. The proposed link from Hugh's Bedford line to the Worcestershire Beauchamps (next generation) is traditional from Sir William Dugdale's seventeenth-century pedigree but not documented in contemporary charters. _Marked as traditional accordingly._ **Surname etymology.** _Beauchamp_ derives from the Old French _Bel-Champ_ — "fine or lovely field" — Latinised in medieval English records as _de Bello Campo_. Common variant spellings: Beecham, Beacham. **The Norman seat: Beauchamps in La Manche.** Multiple sources — the regional Norman authority **Wikimanche**, 19th-century herald J.R. Planché in _Companions of the Conqueror_, and the Battle Abbey Roll commentary at 1066.co.nz — agree that the family's pre-Conquest seat was the commune of **Beauchamps in La Manche** (canton La Haye-Pesnel, dépt Manche, in the Avranchin region between Avranches and Granville), _not_ Beauchamps-en-Bessin near Bayeux as some online genealogies state. Wikimanche calls this commune _"le berceau anglo-normand des Beauchamp devenus comtes de Warwick et de Worcester"_ — the Anglo-Norman cradle of the Beauchamps who became Earls of Warwick and Worcester. 1066.co.nz adds the conjecture that the family was a cadet branch of the **Barons of Saint-Denis-le-Gast**, the regional baronial house. The family was likely _minor-to-middling Norman landed knights_ rather than first-rank ducal magnates: no Beauchamp appears in any surviving Norman ducal charter before 1066, and no Beauchamp is among the ~15 "Proven Companions of William the Conqueror" who can be securely identified at Hastings from contemporary sources. The Battle Abbey Roll and Falaise Roll, both of which list a "Beauchampe," are 14th-century compilations not contemporary 1066 musters. **Three competing reconstructions of his identity.** The historical record around Hugh's exact identity is contested — three traditions are worth knowing: **Wikimanche / regional Norman tradition** — Hugh is the same person as **Hugues I, seigneur de Beauchamps** in La Manche; born **c. 1040**; accompanied William the Conqueror at Hastings in 1066 (about age 26); identified with Domesday's "Hugo de Belcamp" (1086); died after 1100. _One person, fighting age at Hastings._ **Cawley / FMG / peer-reviewed academic** — Hugh born **c. 1050-1060**, deduced from his absence from any William I charter (he comes of age only late in William's reign; first documented under William II Rufus 1087-1100). Under this reading he was **6-16 at Hastings** and almost certainly not a combatant. His parentage is undocumented in surviving primary sources. **Geneanet "irishwhiskey" tradition** — there are two generations: a father **Robert de Beauchamp** (b. c. 1020, d. c. 1066 — possibly at or around Hastings) and his son **Hugh** (b. c. 1040). Under this reading, the Conquest-era combatant is Robert, who dies in 1066, and the young Hugh inherits the English lands. _Speculative — no primary source corroborates Robert as a separate person._ The Wikimanche identification has the merit of _simplicity_ (one person, doing all the documented deeds); the Cawley dating is more rigorous; the Geneanet two-generation tradition explains the 1066 death date attributed to Robert but adds an undocumented generation. This tree shows the Geneanet structure (Robert as Hugh's speculative father) for breadth, but the Wikimanche identification — **Hugues I, seigneur de Beauchamps** = our Hugh — is the regional Norman authority's view. Hugh's 47 manors in Domesday 1086 reflect not pre-Conquest baronial rank but a post-Conquest reward — and probably his marriage to **Matilda Taillebois**, almost certainly daughter and heiress of Ralph Taillebois (sheriff of Bucks before Hugh, who married Hugh into a major Norman tenancy). **Family arms (senior Earls of Warwick branch).** Blazon: _Gules, a fesse between six cross crosslets or_. Crest: _Out of a ducal coronet or, a swan's head gules_. Motto: **Verité sans peur** — "Truth without fear." (Source: H.R.C. Family Name History certificate, supplied by the family.) **Bayeux Tapestry tradition.** Family memory holds that one of the unnamed Norman knights depicted around William the Conqueror in the Bayeux Tapestry may represent Hugh — characterised as a mid-level commander with authority rather than a "right-hand man." The Tapestry names William's chief lieutenants (William fitz Osbern, Bishop Odo, Robert of Mortain) but leaves most of the supporting knights anonymous. There is no surviving documentary evidence that confirms or denies a Hugh identification; the tradition lives on the strength of the family knowing he was at the Conquest. _A note on names._ Family memory sometimes fuses Hugh I (the Domesday tenant-in-chief, d. c. 1100) with his grandson **Hugh II of Bedford** (d. c. 1141, son of Simon). Charters distinguish them: it's Hugh I who held the 43 Bedfordshire manors at Domesday, and Hugh II who was holding Bedford Castle through the Anarchy. **Modern scholarship.** Per Emma Mason's ODNB entry on Walter de Beauchamp, K.S.B. Keats-Rohan's _Domesday Descendants_, and the FMG _Medieval Lands_ project: Hugh's only documented children are **Simon** (d. c. 1137) and **Robert** (d. before 1130). The 17th-century Dugdale reconstruction that places **Walter de Beauchamp of Elmley** as Hugh's third son is _not corroborated by contemporary charters_; Walter's parentage is unknown in surviving records. The Bedford and Elmley/Warwick branches of the Beauchamps may in fact be distinct families. This tree preserves the traditional Dugdale descent for continuity with the Lorraine Shoemaker compilation, but the join between Hugh and Walter is _genealogically unproven_. **The Crusader cousin.** Through Hugh's documented son Robert → grandson Payn de Beauchamp (m. Rohese de Vere, founder of Chicksand Priory) → great-grandson Simon (d. 1207) ran a cadet line that included **Hugh de Beauchamp of Eaton Bray**, who took the cross in 1186, sailed for the Holy Land with **Roger de Mowbray**, and was **killed at the Battle of Hattin on 4 July 1187** — the catastrophic Crusader defeat that triggered the Third Crusade. His wife Philippa de Trailly made a recorded pious benefaction at St. Neots Priory at his departure. (Source: G.H. Fowler, "The Beauchamps, Barons of Eaton," _Beds. Hist. Rec. Soc._ 2, 1914; Christopher Tyerman, _England and the Crusades_, p. 162; the Independent Crusaders Project, Fordham.)

Walter de Beauchamp

of Elmley · Sheriff of Worcestershire
d. 1130–1133
Direct linetraditionalRelation: 27th-great-grandfather
Connections
  • Spouse: Emmeline d'Abetot
  • Incoming links: Hugh / Hugues I de Beauchamp (parent/child)
  • Outgoing links: William de Beauchamp (parent/child); Roger de Beauchamp (side/collateral)
Notes
Born **c. 1065**, died between **1130 and 1133** (last appears in the 1130 Pipe Roll). One of _Henry I's "new men"_ — promoted from obscurity by royal favor rather than great hereditary descent. His parentage is not documented in contemporary records: the traditional 17th-century reconstruction placing him as a son of Hugh de Beauchamp of Bedford is _not corroborated_ by Mason's ODNB study, Keats-Rohan, or the FMG project. Inherited the Worcestershire estates and the hereditary **Sheriffdom of Worcestershire** through his wife Emeline — but the "inheritance" was actually a royal grant after her brother **Roger d'Abetot was exiled by Henry I for murdering a royal servant**. Walter held the shrievalty **c. 1114–1130**; was a **royal dispenser** (precursor to butler) and probably royal forester of Worcestershire. Witnessed royal charters 1108–1111. His widow Adeliza's dower lands were confirmed to him by Henry I 1123-29. A long-running land dispute with the Marmion family over Urse's brother Robert's lands ran into the late 12th century. Three sons: **William** (heir, ancestor of the Earls of Warwick), **Stephen**, and **Walter II**. **Elmley Castle** would remain the principal Beauchamp residence for 158 years.

His appearance with the 1216 reissue is the paper trail of that rehabilitation; by 1217 he was restored to the shrievalty and to royal favour.

Joan of Arc · Trial & Execution at RouenRichard's second tour as **Captain of Rouen and Lieutenant of France** (from 1437, but on the ground in Normandy throughout the late 1420s) placed him at the centre of one of the most contested episodes in European memory: the captivity, trial and burning of **Joan of Arc**. Captured by Burgundians at Compiègne on 23 May 1430 and sold to the English for 10,000 livres in November, Joan was transferred to **Rouen Castle** in late December 1430 — held in the very fortress whose military command Richard had inherited from his Caen and Rouen sieges of 1417–19. The 1431 trial was an _ecclesiastical_ proceeding, not an English court-martial: it was prosecuted by **Bishop Pierre Cauchon of Beauvais** in the name of the Church, with the University of Paris supplying the theological ammunition. Richard's role was that of jailer rather than judge — providing the secure custody and English garrison without which the trial could not have proceeded — but he was unmistakably one of its political guarantors. After Joan signed an abjuration, then resumed male dress, she was condemned as a relapsed heretic and burned at the **Vieux-Marché in Rouen on 30 May 1431**. Her ashes were thrown into the Seine; the verdict was overturned by Pope Callixtus III in 1456 and she was canonised in 1920. Shakespeare's _Henry VI Part I_ — written more than 150 years later — turned Warwick into one of the chief villains of the Joan story; the historians Adam Busiakiewicz and Aaron Manning of Warwick Castle (whose podcast prompted this entry) emphasise that the dramatic version is far from the documentary record. Richard was a working soldier-administrator carrying out the policy of the English crown in occupied France; the theological judgement was Cauchon's and the Sorbonne's, and Joan's rehabilitation a generation later did not target Warwick personally. 📺 **Watch:** Richard Beauchamp · The Trial and Execution of Joan of Arc — WarwickFineArt podcast excerpt by Adam Busiakiewicz & Aaron Manning. Beauchamp Chapel · Patronage of the ArtsRichard kept an **unusually cultured household** for an English magnate. Both his wives — first **Elizabeth Berkeley**, then **Isabel le Despenser** — were educated bibliophiles in their own right, and Richard expanded the family's already-significant manuscript collection (the Beauchamps had been collecting illuminated books since the 14th century, including manuscripts of _Guy of Warwick_). He retained **John Shirley** as his secretary — Shirley was one of the most prolific manuscript-anthologists of fifteenth-century England, and the British Library holds his book of poems amassed for Richard. Tradition holds that **Sir Thomas Malory**, author of _Le Morte d'Arthur_, may have served Richard at the siege of Calais during Henry V's campaigns; the evidence is thin but not impossible. **John Lydgate** presented works to him and wrote a version of the Guy of Warwick story for Richard's daughter Margaret Talbot. Shirley's portfolio also includes a poem said to have been written by Richard himself to his wife from France: a passionate love-lyric beginning _"I cannot half to you complain / that doth my woeful heart strain / with heavy thought and grievous pain / when I not see my fair lady…"_ — chivalry as poetry, not just as armoured combat. The **Beauchamp Chapel** in St Mary's Collegiate Church, Warwick, is the architectural distillation of all of this — and the only surviving high-fidelity glimpse into how Richard's castles must have felt inside (since Warwick Castle's interiors were entirely remodelled in later centuries). Construction began at his death in 1439 per his will (_"there be made a chapel of Our Lady well faire and goodly… within the middle of the chapel I will that mine tomb be made"_) and the building cost **£2,482** — an astronomical sum. The Gothic vaulting echoes Gloucester Cathedral and other West-of-England priories whose masons Richard's family actively recruited. The **gilded-bronze effigy** at the centre of the chapel is unprecedented for a non-royal: outside the royal family the only comparable cast-metal effigies of the period are the Black Prince at Canterbury and Henry VII at Westminster. The makers are documented by name: **William Austin of London** cast the figure, **Bartholomew Lambspring** (a Dutchman) gilded it, **Roger of Webb** — a barber — was retained for anatomical fidelity, and **John Massey** carved the Purbeck-marble base. The effigy's hands are _open_ rather than folded, allowing Richard's gaze to meet the Virgin and Child carved into the ceiling boss directly above. The **stained glass** by **John Prudde**, the Royal Glazier, has been called the most expensive of the medieval period in England. The original centrepiece was destroyed by Cromwellian iconoclasts in the 17th century, but the upper tracery survives — bands of **musical angels** playing organ, harp, lute, and percussion, holding sheets of music that can still be read today. The chapel was always intended as a sounding space: Richard's household chapel had eighteen men and nine choristers, and he patronised composers including **Robert Sherry** (Chapel Royal) and **John Saulsby**, a younger contemporary of John Dunstable who travelled with Richard across Europe (including to the Council of Constance) on £10 a year. 📺 **Watch:** The Beauchamp Chapel · The Patronage of Richard Beauchamp, Earl of Warwick — WarwickFineArt podcast (Adam Busiakiewicz & Aaron Manning, 16 min). In Maurice Keen's _Chivalry_Richard 13th Earl is one of the principal English exemplars in **Maurice Keen's _Chivalry_** (Yale University Press, 1984) — the standard modern study of late-medieval chivalric culture. Keen reproduces two scenes from the Beauchamp Pageant featuring Richard: a joust at Calais in January 1414 (plate 21), and the moment of Richard's prominence at the Battle of Shrewsbury 1403 captioned as his Garter investiture (plate 35). The book situates Richard alongside chivalric brotherhoods like the Order of the Garter, the Order of the Sash (1330), and the Order of the Golden Fleece — Richard's knightly career as a foundational example of the institution. **The Beauchamp Pageant itself** (British Library Cotton MS Julius E IV/3) is a 1485-90 vellum manuscript of **fifty-three pen-and-ink illustrations** tracing Richard's entire life from birth to burial — the most ambitious illustrated biography of any English nobleman of the Middle Ages, commissioned by Richard's daughter Anne Beauchamp (16th Countess of Warwick, wife of Warwick the Kingmaker) about fifty years after his death. The 1914 Roxburghe Club facsimile (ed. Sir William St. John Hope) made the full plate-by-plate sequence widely available.

William Lygon, 7th Earl Beauchamp

KG · Liberal leader · exiled 1931
1872 – 1938
Powick → LygonRelation: ~12th cousin (descendant of Anne Beauchamp of Powick — the Lygons of Madresfield)
Connections
  • Incoming links: William Lygon (parent/child)
  • Outgoing links: William Lygon, 8th Earl Beauchamp (parent/child); Hugh Patrick Lygon (parent/child)
Notes
**Inherited the earldom at age 19** in 1891. By his late 20s he had governed New South Wales (1899–1901, representing Queen Victoria); by his 40s he had served as First Commissioner of Works, Lord President of the Council, Lord Privy Seal, and **Liberal Leader in the House of Lords (1924–1931)**. Knight of the Garter (1914). Chancellor of the University of London. **Lord Warden of the Cinque Ports**, holding court at Walmer Castle. Carried the **Sword of State** at the coronation of King George V — the symbolic weapon of royal authority itself. In 1902 he married **Lady Lettice Grosvenor**, sister of Hugh "Bendor" Grosvenor, 2nd Duke of Westminster — a match hailed as the merging of two great houses. Seven children followed: William (Viscount Elmley, later 8th Earl), Hugh Patrick, Lady Mary, Lady Dorothy ("Coot"), Richard, Lettice, and Sibell. Photographs from the 1910s and 20s show a family that radiated stability — picnics, pony rides, theatricals at Madresfield. The 7th Earl was unusual among his peers in temperament: deeply religious, an Anglo-Catholic ritualist, and a patron of the **Arts and Crafts movement**. He commissioned the Madresfield Chapel — its painted angels and elaborate Arts-and-Crafts detailing now considered one of the most beautiful small chapels in England. He cared about embroidery, interior design, the placement of vases. The hard-shooting, hunting-obsessed peers of his class found his manner effeminate. He was also gay. In late-Victorian and Edwardian England this was both a criminal offence (under the 1885 Labouchere Amendment) and a fast track to social ruin — but for men of his rank, an unspoken aristocratic tolerance held, provided one was discreet. The footmen at Madresfield were often hired for their looks; the male-only weekend parties at Walmer were legendary. For decades the open secret stayed open. In 1930, by then 58, he travelled to Australia accompanied by his son Hugh Lygon and the young Liberal MP **Robert Bernays** — a relationship that, in the close confines of a long sea voyage, drew comment from fellow passengers and led to dispatches reaching London. His brother-in-law **Bendor, 2nd Duke of Westminster** — a brutally reactionary Tory who had hated Beauchamp for years on grounds personal, political and aesthetic — used those dispatches as the catalyst. Westminster hired private detectives, who interviewed dismissed servants and built a forensic dossier of named partners, dates, and specific encounters. He took it directly to **King George V**, whose reported reaction — _"I thought men like that shot themselves"_ — became the defining quotation of the scandal. Compounding the king's fury was a second concern: the king's fourth son, **Prince George, Duke of Kent**, was a frequent visitor to Madresfield and had been seriously courting Lady Mary Lygon, the 7th Earl's daughter. The king feared a public trial would drag the bisexual Duke of Kent into the witness box. The decision was therefore not to prosecute openly but to erase: a **warrant for arrest was prepared** but held back, and the 7th Earl was given an ultimatum on **8 June 1931** — leave England by midnight or be arrested. He fled by the night ferry from Dover. The Liberal Party did not defend him; the establishment closed ranks; his clubs went silent. Westminster then turned on his sister Lettice, presented her with the dossier, removed her from Madresfield, controlled her correspondence, and forced her to file for divorce. **The seven Lygon children mutinied.** They sided with their exiled father. Lady Dorothy ("Coot") and Lady Mary led a defiant social campaign in London, refusing to speak to Westminster, returning his gifts, making it clear that to be a friend of theirs you had to acknowledge their father. Lettice died in 1936, estranged from her children. Hugh Lygon died in Germany the same year, age 31, after a roadside collapse — alcoholism finishing what the scandal had started. The 7th Earl mourned both deaths from exile. He wandered Germany, France, Italy, returned briefly to Australia, then settled into restless movement — Venice, Paris, Salzburg — followed by clandestine visits from his children. Cancer was advancing. He died at the **Hotel Gotham, New York City, on 14 November 1938, aged 66**. The warrant for his arrest died with him. His body was returned to Madresfield for burial in the family plot. There was no state funeral; no royal representative attended; the obituaries spoke of "health reasons." The children buried him themselves, against the establishment's lingering disapproval. Madresfield Court — your link The 7th Earl's seat was **Madresfield Court** in the Malvern Hills of Worcestershire — a moated medieval manor that has been continuously occupied by the same family for nine hundred years. Two threads tie Madresfield to your line: _Thread one — Urse d'Abetot._ In 1086 the manor was held by Urse d'Abetot, Sheriff of Worcestershire — your **28× great-grandfather** through Walter de Beauchamp's marriage to Urse's daughter Emmeline (Generation 2). Madresfield itself spun off elsewhere; by 1419 it was held by the Lygon family. _Thread two — the Powick branch._ Three centuries later, your medieval ancestor William 9th Earl of Warwick had a younger son who founded the Powick branch. That cadet line eventually produced **Anne Beauchamp**, daughter of Richard 2nd Baron Beauchamp of Powick, who married Sir Richard Lygon — bringing Beauchamp blood (and the seed of the surname) back into the Lygon family at Madresfield. In 1815 William Lygon was created **Earl Beauchamp**, formally reviving the name through that 15th-century descent. The family in _Brideshead Revisited_ — Evelyn Waugh's Marchmains and Flytes — is based on this Lygon-Beauchamp household at Madresfield, though Waugh changed the patriarch's exile reason from homosexuality to adultery to make the book publishable in 1945. The literary "Beauchamps" of twentieth-century England are your distant cousins, descended from the same medieval root. 📺 _For the full story see "Earl Beauchamp: The Gay Aristocrat Who Disgusted King George V" — Fantastic History on YouTube (73 min)._
